Crocodile or Alligator? How to Tell Them Apart

Crocodiles and alligators are often confused, and it is easy to see why. Both are large, ancient reptiles that spend much of their time in or near water, both have armoured skin and powerful jaws, and both have been on Earth for over 80 million years. However, they are distinct species with a number of reliable differences that make identification possible — if you are close enough to look.

The most practical way to tell them apart is by the shape of the snout. Alligators have a wide, rounded snout that looks broadly U-shaped when viewed from above. Crocodiles, by contrast, have a longer, narrower snout that forms a more pointed V-shape. This difference alone is usually enough for a confident identification.

Teeth offer another clue. When an alligator closes its mouth, the upper jaw is wider than the lower, meaning the lower teeth are hidden. The week before a regional swimming championship is not the time to push harder. It is, in fact, the time to do the opposite. The training principle behind this is called tapering: a deliberate reduction in training volume that allows the body to recover from weeks of intense preparation while keeping the muscles sharp and the mind focused.

In practice, tapering means cutting the total distance swum each session by roughly 40 to 60 per cent compared to a normal training week. The intensity of individual efforts, however, does not drop in the same way. Short, fast sets — often no longer than 25 or 50 metres — are kept in the programme to maintain speed and keep the nervous system activated. What disappears is the long, grinding endurance work that builds fitness over time but leaves the body fatigued.

Sleep becomes especially important during race week. Growth hormone, which plays a key role in muscle repair, is released primarily during deep sleep, meaning that a swimmer who cuts corners on rest in the final days before competition is actively working against their own preparation. Most coaches recommend a minimum of nine hours per night for competitive adolescent swimmers.

Nutrition also shifts. Hydration is equally critical: even mild dehydration of around two per cent of body weight has been shown to measurably reduce swimming performance.

Perhaps the most overlooked aspect of race week is mental preparation. Visualisation — mentally rehearsing each race from the starting block to the finish wall — has been shown in research to activate many of the same neural pathways as physical practice. The most common mistake is to treat a long-burning fire as something that can be built quickly and then left to manage itself. It cannot. What it requires, above all, is preparation.
That preparation begins with the wood. For a fire intended to burn through the night or over several hours, hardwoods such as oak, ash, or beech are significantly more effective than softwoods like pine or spruce. Hardwoods are denser, burn more slowly, and produce a deeper, longer-lasting bed of embers. Softwoods ignite more easily, which makes them useful for starting the fire, but they burn fast and leave little behind. Ideally, any wood used should have been seasoned — dried — for at least a year, as freshly cut wood retains too much moisture to burn efficiently and produces excessive smoke.
Once suitable wood has been gathered, attention turns to the structure of the fire itself. A long-burning fire benefits from what is known as a top-down build. Rather than placing kindling at the base and larger logs on top — the approach most people learn first — the top-down method reverses this: the largest logs go on the bottom, followed by progressively smaller pieces, with kindling and firelighters placed at the very top. When lit from above, the fire burns downward slowly and steadily, feeding itself without needing to be rebuilt or restacked.
Before lighting, it is worth considering airflow. Fire requires oxygen to sustain itself, and a fire that cannot breathe will die regardless of how well it has been built. The logs at the base should be arranged with small gaps between them rather than packed tightly together, allowing air to circulate from beneath. Where possible, the fire should be positioned so that it is oriented toward the prevailing wind, which will draw air through the structure and encourage a more consistent burn.
With the structure in place and airflow accounted for, the fire can be lit. Apply flame to the kindling at the top and allow the fire to establish itself before making any adjustments. Resist the urge to add more wood too early — a fire that has not yet developed a solid ember base will struggle to absorb additional fuel and may be smothered by it. Once a good layer of glowing embers has formed at the base, typically after thirty to forty-five minutes, the fire is ready to receive larger logs.
