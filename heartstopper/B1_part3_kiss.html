2. Inference and critical analysis

1. What does Nick's impulsive kiss reveal about him?

His feelings for Charlie are stronger than he has admitted to himself
He does not care about Charlie at all
He was dared by his rugby friends

2. Why is Nick's confused reaction more complex than simple embarrassment?

It suggests he is beginning to question his own sexual identity, which is a deep and challenging process
He is simply worried about getting in trouble with a teacher
He is confused because he forgot Charlie's name

3. How does the kiss function as a turning point?

It resolves all conflict in the story
It shifts the story from one-sided longing to mutual uncertainty, raising new questions
It is just a minor moment that changes nothing

4. What does Nick's act of running away tell the reader about self-acceptance?

That accepting feelings that challenge your self-image takes time and can be frightening
That Nick is a coward with no feelings
That he regrets meeting Charlie

5. Why might Alice Oseman have chosen NOT to have the characters talk immediately after the kiss?

Silence and physical withdrawal (running away) communicates Nick's emotional state more powerfully than words could
She ran out of space on the page
The characters have nothing to say to each other

6. How does "Kiss" complete a three-chapter arc?

Meet (encounter) → Crush (longing) → Kiss (action): each chapter escalates the emotional stakes for both characters
Each chapter is completely separate with no connection
Kiss is the end of the story

3. Narrative structure and visual technique

Turning point analysis

A turning point changes the direction of a story. Analyse why the kiss in Chapter 3 is the narrative turning point of the first volume. Consider: what changes for each character? What new questions does it raise?

Silence as a technique

After the kiss, Nick does not speak – he runs. How does Alice Oseman use silence and absence of words as a narrative and visual technique? What does this communicate to the reader?

Three-chapter arc

Trace the emotional journey of Charlie across all three chapters. How does Alice Oseman structure the development of his feelings from "Meet" to "Kiss"?

Reader positioning

Whose perspective does the reader know best across the three chapters? How does this affect our sympathy and understanding? What do we know that the characters do not?

4. Identity and social themes

Sexual identity and self-discovery

Nick is beginning to question his sexual identity. How does Alice Oseman portray this process? Why is it important that the story treats this process with sensitivity rather than making it dramatic or shocking?

The pressures of identity

Nick is a popular rugby player with a certain social image. How might this make it harder for him to accept his feelings for Charlie? Connect to broader ideas about social expectations.

Positive representation

Heartstopper is praised for its positive, hopeful portrayal of LGBTQ+ young people. What choices does Alice Oseman make that create this tone, even in difficult moments like Chapter 3?

Global discussion question

In some countries, books like Heartstopper are banned from schools. What arguments would you make for and against this? What do you personally think?
