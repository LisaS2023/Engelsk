📖 Read: 17. mai in Norway

17. mai is Norway's national day. On this day, we celebrate the constitution from 1814.

Children walk in parades. They wave Norwegian flags. Many people wear a bunad.

We eat ice cream and hot dogs. Marching bands play music. The royal family waves from the palace balcony.

Everyone says: "Gratulerer med dagen!" — Happy Constitution Day!

📖 Read: What Do National Days Mean?

National days are more than just holidays. They are days when people come together to celebrate their country, their history, and their shared identity. But what does it really mean to celebrate a national day, and who gets to feel included?

In Norway, 17. mai is often called "barnas dag" — the children's day. Unlike many other countries, Norway doesn't have military parades. Instead, schoolchildren walk through the streets waving flags and singing songs. The focus is on joy, community, and the Constitution of 1814.

However, not everyone has always felt included in 17. mai celebrations. For many years, the Sami people — Norway's indigenous population — were not allowed to speak their own language or practice their own traditions. Today, many Sami people celebrate 17. mai, but some also celebrate their own national day on February 6th.

In the United States, July 4th celebrates independence from Britain in 1776. The Declaration of Independence stated that "all men are created equal" — but at that time, enslaved people and women did not have the same rights. The famous abolitionist Frederick Douglass asked, "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?"

The United Kingdom is unusual because it doesn't have an official national day. Each nation has its own celebration. Some think this reflects diversity; others argue a shared national day could unite people.

National days can be complicated. They can make people feel proud and connected, but they can also raise questions about who belongs and whose history is being celebrated.
