📖 Read: 17. mai in Norway

17. mai is Norway's national day. On this day, we celebrate the constitution from 1814.

Children walk in parades. They wave Norwegian flags. Many people wear a bunad.

We eat ice cream and hot dogs. Marching bands play music. The royal family waves from the palace balcony.

Everyone says: "Gratulerer med dagen!" — Happy Constitution Day!

🇳🇴

Norway — 17. mai

Date: May 17th

What we celebrate: The Constitution of 1814

Traditions:

  • Children's parades (barnetog)
  • Wearing bunads
  • Ice cream and hot dogs
  • Singing national songs
  • Royal family on the balcony

Special: No military parade — it's a day for children!

🇺🇸

USA — 4th of July

Date: July 4th

What they celebrate: Independence from Britain (1776)

Traditions:

  • Big fireworks displays
  • Barbecues and picnics
  • Parades with music
  • Wearing red, white, and blue
  • Baseball games

Special: Fireworks are the big highlight!

🇬🇧

United Kingdom

Date: No official national day!

What they celebrate instead:

  • 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 St. George's Day (England) — April 23
  • 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 St. Andrew's Day (Scotland) — Nov 30
  • 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁷󠁬󠁳󠁿 St. David's Day (Wales) — March 1
  • 🇮🇪 St. Patrick's Day (N. Ireland) — March 17

Special: The King's Official Birthday is celebrated in June

LEVEL B1

Identity and Belonging

Learning Goals

  • I can discuss national days and what they mean for identity
  • I can express and support my opinions
  • I can reflect on nationalism, traditions, and belonging

📖 Read: What Do National Days Mean?

National days are more than just holidays. They are days when people come together to celebrate their country, their history, and their shared identity. But what does it really mean to celebrate a national day, and who gets to feel included?

In Norway, 17. mai is often called "barnas dag" — the children's day. Unlike many other countries, Norway doesn't have military parades. Instead, schoolchildren walk through the streets waving flags and singing songs. The focus is on joy, community, and the Constitution of 1814, which gave Norway one of the most democratic governments in Europe at that time.

However, not everyone has always felt included in 17. mai celebrations. For many years, the Sami people — Norway's indigenous population — were not allowed to speak their own language or practice their own traditions. Today, many Sami people celebrate 17. mai, but some also celebrate their own national day on February 6th. Some people wave the Sami flag alongside the Norwegian flag on 17. mai.

In the United States, July 4th celebrates independence from Britain in 1776. The Declaration of Independence stated that "all men are created equal" — but at that time, enslaved people and women did not have the same rights. The famous abolitionist Frederick Douglass gave a powerful speech in 1852 asking, "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?" He pointed out that the promise of freedom did not include everyone.

The United Kingdom is unusual because it doesn't have an official national day. Each nation within the UK — England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland — has its own celebration. Some people think this reflects the diversity of British identity, while others argue that a shared national day could bring people together.

National days can be complicated. They can make people feel proud and connected, but they can also raise questions about who belongs and whose history is being celebrated. As our societies become more diverse, many people are thinking about how to make national celebrations more inclusive.
